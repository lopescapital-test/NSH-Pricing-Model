§1 · The Three Tiers

How much do you want us walking you through it?

The tier ladder is built on one parent-comprehensible question: how much guided support do you want during the 12-week intensive? The protocol is the same. The kit is the same. What changes is how much we're with you while you execute it.

The three philosophies in one line each

Foundation: "We'll teach you, then you run with it."

Heavy front-load in Week 1. Weekly clinical check-ins after. Most of the protocol execution happens solo with periodic coaching. Best for families who learn fast and want autonomy.

Accelerated: "A healthy mix of guided and at-home work."

Multiple weekly clinical and concierge touchpoints during active phase. Mixed model — clinician adjusts, concierge coaches, parent executes the rest. The recommended path for most families.

Immersive: "We'll be with you every day, walking you through it."

Daily M-F concierge sessions during the active phase. Twice-weekly clinician sessions. Bi-weekly Kyle involvement. Maximum guided support for the most complex cases or families who want premium clinical density.

Why this framing works. Parents don't want to be told their child is a "Profile B HIGH-intensity case." They want to know how much hand-holding they're getting. The internal profile-to-tier mapping drives the concierge's recommendation; the family chooses based on philosophy alignment and budget capacity.

§4 · Retention Discount

The discount is a retention tool, not a haircut

$1,500 / $2,000 / $2,500 are not sticker discounts. They are credits applied to Continuing Care fees after the 12-week intensive completes and the family enrolls in CC. Families pay sticker upfront. They earn the discount by staying on the platform.

Why this structure

Sticker stays clean. LTV pays for retention.

A sticker discount tells the family "we're worth less than we said." A retention credit tells them "we'll be here for the long haul, and we'll prove it by lowering your ongoing cost when you stay with us." First framing erodes pricing power. Second framing builds relationship debt that pays off in CC retention months.

Mechanically: intensive revenue books at full sticker — $9,500 / $15,000 / $19,000. Intensive margin (61.8% / 64.5% / 68.1% pilot) is what hits the P&L at Wk 12. The discount is a contingent liability that converts to a CC-fee offset only if the family enrolls in CC and continues using the platform.

How the credit works

At Wk 12 completion and CC enrollment, the discount amount is credited to the family's CC account. Applied against monthly CC fees until exhausted. If CC ends early, remaining credit lapses. If the family never enrolls in CC, the discount never costs the company anything.

Why the discount grows with tier. Higher-tier families have higher LTV expectations: more touch-points to retain, more reason to commit, more revenue at stake if they churn. $1,500 / $2,000 / $2,500 maps to that LTV gradient — entry tier rewards retention modestly, premium tier offers a bigger carrot to stay. Discount as % of sticker is monotonic: 15.8% / 13.3% / 13.2% — slightly bigger relative incentive at Foundation, where price sensitivity is highest.
Margin floor consideration. If 100% of families claim the full discount, effective margins compress below 60% on Foundation and Accelerated. This is acceptable because: ① the discount is gated on CC enrollment, which means the family is still on the platform generating CC revenue at its own margin; ② not all families will complete the 12 weeks or enroll in CC, so realized discount take-up is less than 100%; ③ the discount displaces revenue we wouldn't otherwise capture (most families don't enroll in CC at full price). CC pricing and CC margin are modeled separately — see Continuing Care Pricing v1.0 (forthcoming).
